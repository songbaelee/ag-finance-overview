The Landscape

Who actually supplies the capital that reaches agri-SMEs today? This page maps the landscape by approach — demand side vs. supply side — and names real organizations as examples of each.

Figures below are restated from Market Sizing — see that page for the full report citation and breakdown.

Recap: the gap this page is about

$74B Annual financing gap
~$14B Formal supply currently met
~16% Share of demand met

Approaches & named examples Illustrative

The categorization below is our own — the market-sizing report doesn't segment supply this way. Named organizations are real, illustrative examples of each approach, not an exhaustive list or a claim about market share. This is a first pass: structure only, no drag or toggle interactivity yet, and descriptions are kept deliberately minimal.

Demand side

Demand side (assume: grants only)
  • Farmer level (agricultural extension)
    One Acre Fund

    Delivers bundled inputs, credit, and training directly to smallholder farmers — grant-funded technical assistance at the farmer level.

  • Firm level
    Agri Frontier

    Advisory support aimed at the agribusiness firm itself — helping build the systems and track record a firm needs to become financeable.

    Also appears under Supply side → Donor-directed → More control, in its advisory-arm capacity, and is reportedly building its own fund — see FASA under Supply side → Funds → Grant.

Supply side

Supply side
  • Banks
    • Guarantee
      DFC

      U.S. International Development Finance Corporation — issues guarantees that reduce the risk banks take on when lending to agribusiness borrowers.

    • Grant-to-incentivize
      Aceli

      Pays lenders performance-based incentives to make more (and better-priced) loans to agri-SMEs, rather than guaranteeing the loans directly.

  • Funds
    • Grant
      FASA

      A fund that channels grant capital toward expanding agri-SME lending, rather than lending its own repayable capital.

      Managed by I&P, whose advisory arm resembles Agri Frontier's work. N3F, one of the donor entities behind vehicles like this one, is listed separately under Supply side → Donor-directed → Less control.

    • Repayable
      • SME-specialist
        Balloon

        Invests in small and growing businesses broadly — agriculture is one sector among several, not an agriculture-only mandate.

      • Agri-specialist
        • Local
          Pearl Capital Partners

          An Africa-based fund manager investing specifically in agribusiness SMEs across the region.

        • International
          • Lending-focus
            SIDI

            An international solidarity-finance investor providing debt to agricultural and rural enterprises.

          • TA-focus
            Root Capital

            Pairs lending to agricultural enterprises with substantial technical assistance, rather than capital alone.

  • Donor-directed (ordered by control retained, most → least)
    • Most control
      Isidro (balance-sheet lending)

      Lends directly from its own balance sheet — the donor-directed structure that retains the most direct control over capital deployment.

    • More control
      Daraja

      A donor-directed vehicle retaining more control over deployment than a hands-off fund, less than direct balance-sheet lending.

      Agri Frontier (advisory side)

      The same organization listed under Demand side → Firm level, here in its advisory-arm role rather than as a firm-level TA provider.

      See Firm level above.

    • Less control, narrow strategy
      N3F

      A donor-directed vehicle aimed at a narrow, defined strategy rather than close control of individual deals.

      Also referenced under Supply side → Funds → Grant, as one of the donor entities behind grant-funded vehicles like FASA.

      One Acre Ventures (JV with IDH)

      A joint venture pursuing a defined strategy rather than direct deal-by-deal control.
